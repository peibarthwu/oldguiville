What is Guiville?

Guiville is a city within a graphical user interface or GUI, a visual language developed in the late 1970s-early 1980s that made navigating through a file system easier. All the roads and houses in Guiville may be moved by anyone, which is a HUGE inconvenience, because sometimes a citizen might find that all the roads are suddenly rearranged (or worse, their entire house is moved).

Guiville imagines how user interfaces (which are generally supposed to increase convenience and make navigation easier) could be incredibly incovenient and make navigation difficult. (What if a citizen's entire house gets moved by someone playing around with Guiville from a computer?)

More broadly, this project explores urban planning and development, space and runtime constraints, acoustic ecology, and life on the internet.

What's going on in Guiville?

It is currently an election year in Guiville, and the citizens are voting whether to abolish their monarchy. There are protests because the ballot center is just a folder and anyone on the internet can click on a ballot and view how a citizen voted. The oligarchy is attempting to gerrymander Guiville, which is pointless, because the houses are moved regularly by people navigating the interface.

The political unrest in 2D Guiville has prompted the wealthy citizens to pursue the production of St. Guis, a 3D city. The entry point to St. Guis sits beyond the Great Paywall, and all property there is constrained by a polygon quota.

Most of the citizens don't know it, but there is a way to break past the bounds of the city into the seemingly infinite GUI. This discovery remains a secret, although a Gui-villa in the expansive territory implies that someone lives out there, and have chosen to hide their findings from the rest of the city. Removed from the political turmoil of Guiville, the inhabitants of the Gui-villa put their effort into attempting to communicate with you, posing the question: what is your relationship to Guiville as you view it through a computer?

Blueprint ads for St. Guis. The addition on the third dimension is partially inspired by Edwin Abbott Abbott's Flatland .

Alluding partially to Blue Origin (Jeff Bezos' space travel company), the secret portion of the city belongs to someone who uses their unique access to resources to pursue their metaphysical interests, with little regard for the city's turmoil. The soundscape changes upon entry to this part of the city, which may be more easily discovered depending on the size of the viewing device. In this way, the real-life resources of visitors to the city changes the level of difficulty involved in discovering the villa.
